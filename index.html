The technology stack, layer by layer

01
Document Ingestion
3,232 county profiles loaded into LightRAG

Each county document contains structured data from five federal sources: FEMA National Risk Index (risk scores for 18 hazard types), CDC Social Vulnerability Index, CDC PLACES health outcomes, Census ACS socioeconomic indicators, and FEMA Major Disaster Declaration history. LightRAG ingests these as text and extracts entities and relationships automatically using an LLM.

02
Graph Construction
Entities and relationships extracted and indexed

LightRAG reads each document and extracts named entities (county names, hazard types, index names) and the relationships between them. These become nodes and edges in a graph database. When you see Tama → Wildfire in the graph, that's a stored relationship with associated risk data. "Degree 19" means Tama County has 19 direct connections — one for each hazard type and risk index it's linked to.

03
Hybrid Retrieval
Graph traversal + vector similarity search combined

When you ask a question, LightRAG runs two searches simultaneously: (1) traverses the graph to find relevant entities and their neighbors, and (2) runs vector similarity search over the raw document chunks. Combining both results is what allows it to answer specific factual questions ("what is Tama County's wildfire risk?") and complex comparative questions ("which Iowa counties have high flood risk and low health scores?").

04
AI Synthesis
Claude generates grounded answers from retrieved context

The retrieved graph context is passed to Claude with your question. The model synthesizes a natural language answer from the graph data — not from general training knowledge. This is Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG): the AI is grounded in your specific dataset. Answers include citations back to source counties and federal datasets.

05
API + Spatial RAG
Any app can query the graph — including ArcGIS maps

LightRAG exposes a REST API. Any application can send a natural language question and receive a structured answer. The next evolution is Spatial RAG: connecting this graph to ArcGIS maps so a disaster planner can click a county on a map and ask "what's this county's compounded risk profile?" — answered in seconds by the knowledge graph, displayed directly in the map interface.

The data behind the graph

Every node in this knowledge graph represents a real federal, academic, or civil society dataset. Here's what each one is and why it matters for disaster response.

●
FEMA National Risk Index
18 natural hazard risk scores per county

FEMA's NRI scores every US county on 18 natural hazards — wildfire, hurricane, flood, tornado, earthquake, drought, and more. Each score combines expected annual loss with social vulnerability and community resilience. It's the closest thing to a single authoritative "how dangerous is this county" number. Used by FEMA for grant allocation and by Red Cross for regional planning.

●
CDC Social Vulnerability Index (SVI)
Which communities struggle most to recover

The CDC SVI measures a community's ability to withstand and recover from external stresses like disasters. It combines 16 Census variables across four themes: socioeconomic status, household composition, race/ethnicity/language, and housing/transportation. A high SVI score means a community has less capacity to prepare, respond, and recover — even if the physical hazard risk is the same as a wealthier neighbor.

●
CDC PLACES
County-level health outcomes across America

CDC PLACES provides small-area estimates of chronic disease prevalence, health behaviors, and health outcomes for every US county. This matters for disaster response because a county with high rates of asthma, diabetes, or heart disease has a population that is far more vulnerable to wildfire smoke, heat waves, and displacement. Medical needs during shelter operations can be anticipated from PLACES data before a disaster strikes.

●
ALICE (United Way)
Asset Limited, Income Constrained, Employed

ALICE households earn above the federal poverty line but cannot afford basic necessities — housing, food, childcare, transportation, healthcare. They are the working poor, invisible to most federal vulnerability metrics. During disasters, ALICE households cannot self-evacuate, cannot absorb a week without income, and cannot recover without assistance. Yet they rarely qualify for traditional aid programs. United Way's ALICE data fills a critical gap that CDC SVI and Census poverty measures miss entirely.

●
NOAA Climate Projections COMING SOON
Future hazard risk, not just historical

Current FEMA NRI scores are based on historical loss data. NOAA climate projections add a forward-looking layer — where wildfire risk is expanding, where flood zones are shifting, where hurricane intensity is increasing. For Red Cross pre-positioning and chapter boundary decisions, planning against tomorrow's risk profile rather than yesterday's is the difference between reactive and proactive humanitarian response.

●
211 Call Data (United Way) COMING SOON
Real-time community need signals

211 is the national helpline for health and human services. When disasters strike, 211 call volume spikes — for food, shelter, utilities, and transportation. This data is a real-time signal of where ALICE households are in crisis. Layered against FEMA declarations and SVI scores, 211 call patterns reveal which counties are underserved by existing aid infrastructure and where Red Cross capacity gaps are most acute.

●
HRSA Healthcare Access COMING SOON
Medical deserts in high-risk counties

HRSA designates Health Professional Shortage Areas — counties and regions with insufficient primary care, dental, or mental health providers. A county with Very High wildfire risk, poor CDC PLACES health outcomes, and an HRSA shortage designation is a triple-threat for disaster medical response. Shelter medical planning, mutual aid routing, and pre-positioned medical supplies all become more precise when healthcare access data is layered into the risk picture.

●
Wikipedia County Articles COMING SOON
Narrative context for every county

Structured federal data tells you a county's risk score. It doesn't tell you that Terrebonne Parish has been fighting coastal erosion for a century, that Butte County's Camp Fire destroyed an entire town, or that Tama County is home to the Meskwaki Nation's Settlement. Wikipedia's county articles add the narrative layer — history, economy, demographics, geography, notable events — that transforms raw scores into contextual intelligence. All 3,232 county articles are being ingested now.
